The purple economy: turning a lavender farm into a destination

A small farm doesn't break even by selling raw flowers — it breaks even by turning them into products people carry home and experiences people drive for. This is the plan for the revenue side: value-added goods, visitors, and the brand that ties them together.

The core idea

For a small farm, the margin isn't in the crop — it's in what you do with it

Raw lavender is a commodity. The same acre becomes a living once you climb the value ladder and invite people onto the land. Industry experience is blunt about it: for small lavender farms, agritourism + direct-market value-added products is the most profitable model, and diversifying this way has lifted farm revenue by around 25%.

Sell the jar, not the stem

Value-added products

Processing raw flowers into shelf-stable goods multiplies margin and lets you sell year-round, far beyond the ~6-week bloom.

Sell the visit

Agritourism

U-pick, tours, and events turn the farm itself into the product — visitors pay to be there and buy on the way out.

Sell the feeling

Brand & story

A calm, beautiful, authentic farm is a marketing asset. The lifestyle you want is the thing customers are buying.

Reframe: you're not a lavender grower who also sells things — you're a small hospitality-and-products business that happens to grow its own hero ingredient.

Location is doing your marketing

You'd be buying into the best-known lavender address in the country

Sequim brands itself the "Lavender Capital of North America," and its summer festival is a decades-old visitor magnet. A new farm here inherits an audience that already exists — a rare head start.

The anchor event

Sequim Lavender Weekend

The 30th annual festival runs July 17–19, 2026, centered on Carrie Blake Park, with farms across the Dungeness Valley opening for tours, u-pick, and sales.

Built-in trail

An established farm circuit

Roughly a dozen grower-association farms already draw visitors farm-to-farm — a marketing cooperative you can join rather than build from scratch.

Reasons to come

A tourism corridor

Dungeness Spit, Olympic National Park & Hurricane Ridge, and Port Angeles keep a steady stream of travelers passing your gate all season.

Action: join the Sequim Lavender Growers Association / trail early — membership plugs a new farm into the festival map, shared advertising, and existing visitor traffic.

The distillation math (illustrative): an acre yields ~1–2 gallons of essential oil. Sold wholesale that's ~$3,000/gallon — but bottled into 10 ml retail bottles at ~$15 each, a gallon's worth can fetch on the order of $50,000. The lesson isn't "get rich on oil" — it's small packages sold direct beat bulk sold wholesale, every time.

Revenue stream 2 · Visitors

The farm as the attraction — ways people pay to be there

Come pick

U-pick fields

Visitors cut their own bundles — low labor for you, memorable for them, and they buy products after.

Come learn

Tours & workshops

Free or ticketed farm tours; paid classes in wreath-making, distillation, or sachets.

Come sit

Farm stand & café treats

A shop plus signature bites — lavender lemonade and ice cream are proven crowd-pleasers here.

Come celebrate

Events & photos

Small weddings, photography sessions, and private group visits — high value, few dates.

Illustrative scale: a modest u-pick drawing ~2,000 visitors at ~$25 average spend is about $50,000 in a season — layered on top of product sales, not instead of them.
Aging-friendly design

Lean toward self-guided tours, clear signage, and seasonal help during the bloom rush. The goal is to host the crowd for a few intense weeks, then return to a quiet farm — not to run a theme park year-round.

Where you actually sell

A mix of channels keeps cash flowing past the bloom

Highest margin

On-farm stand & shop

No middleman, full retail price, impulse buys from tour visitors. Your best channel by far.

Reach

Online store + shipping

Shelf-stable products travel well; a simple web shop sells to festival visitors long after they've gone home.

Foot traffic

Farmers markets & festival

Seasonal direct sales plus the marketing value of showing up where buyers already gather.

Volume

Wholesale to local shops

Gift stores, tasting rooms, and inns — lower margin, but steady and hands-off once set up.

Partnerships

Amenity soaps, spa oils, culinary lavender — recurring orders tied to the local tourism economy.

Subscriptions & gifts

Seasonal gift boxes and a small subscription smooth revenue across the quiet months.

Principle: anchor on the on-farm shop (best margin), use online + wholesale to sell the off-season, and treat the festival as your once-a-year customer-acquisition engine.

The rulebook · Products

Two licenses define how far your products can travel

The on-ramp

WA Cottage Food Permit

Make low-risk foods in your home kitchen and sell them directly to consumers — farm stand, farmers market, festival. About $355 for two years, capped at $35,000 gross sales/year.

The scale-up

WSDA Food Processor License

Required to wholesale or sell through stores/restaurants. Fees scale with sales — roughly $92 under $50K, rising by tier. Needs an approved processing facility.

Big caveat: soap, lotion, balms, and essential oils are cosmetics, not food — the cottage-food permit doesn't cover them. They fall under separate FDA cosmetic rules. Keep your edible and body-care product lines on different regulatory tracks.
Sensible path

Start with a cottage-food + on-farm footprint to test products cheaply, then graduate to a processor license once wholesale or online volume justifies a commercial kitchen.

The rulebook · Visitors

Inviting the public brings two legal chores — both very manageable

Liability

Post the sign, get the immunity

Washington's agritourism law (RCW 4.24.830–.850) shields you from liability for the inherent risks of farm visits — but only if you post the specific warning sign at the main entry. It won't cover gross negligence, known hazards you hid, or serving alcohol. Carry farm liability insurance regardless.

Zoning

Check Clallam County first

Counties decide whether agritourism is allowed outright or needs a conditional use permit. State growth law treats it as an accessory farm use as long as you convert no more than an acre out of production (RCW 36.70A.177) — verify the local rules before planning events.

Pre-opening checklist: confirm Clallam County zoning / permit status → post the compliant RCW warning sign → bind a farm liability insurance policy. Do these three and the public-facing side is on solid ground.

The thread that ties it together

Build one brand and let it run across everything

A lavender farm sells beauty and calm — your marketing just has to be as consistent as the product. The same look and story should carry from the bottle label to the front gate to the website.

Identity

Name, mark & palette

One farm name, one logo, one color story (the purple rows sell themselves) — applied to labels, signage, and packaging alike.

Presence

Site + online shop + maps

A simple website with a shop, plus Google Maps and tourism-trail listings so travelers can find and buy from you.

Reach

Photography & social

The farm is endlessly photogenic — visitor photos and a light social presence are cheap, compounding advertising.

Your unfair advantage: the authentic, unhurried lifestyle you're designing for yourself is exactly the story customers want to buy into. Live it, photograph it, and the brand writes itself.

Start during the runway

What to do now — years before you plant a thing

  • Visit as research. Spend a Lavender Weekend walking the Sequim farms — note what they sell, charge, and how they host.
  • Meet the growers' association. Understand membership, the festival, and shared marketing before you're a competitor-neighbor.
  • Draft a product line. Pick a starter set across the ladder — a few culinary, a few body-care, dried goods — and price them.
  • Map the license path. Cottage-food to start, WSDA processor license later; keep food vs. cosmetics separate.
  • Pre-clear the public side. Note Clallam zoning, the RCW warning sign, and a farm liability policy for opening day.
  • Claim the brand early. A name, a domain, and a simple page can start gathering an audience long before bloom one.

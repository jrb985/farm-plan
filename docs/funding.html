The next slide answers your actual question head-on: is there money in buying land and not farming it — or in growing wheat instead of lavender?

Your question, answered

"Is it better to buy land and not farm it — or grow wheat for the subsidies?"

Buy & hold lightly — yes, real money

Three programs literally pay you to keep land out of intensive production: enroll sensitive acres in CRP for annual rent, sell development rights via a conservation easement, and drop the parcel into current-use tax valuation. All three reward owning green land, not maximizing yield.

Wheat / commodities — not your game

Commodity subsidies (crop insurance, ARC/PLC price supports) exist, but they require actually farming grain at scale on thin margins, tie you to machinery and acreage, and reward volume — the opposite of a low-stress, aging-friendly, break-even farm.

The counter-intuitive truth: for a small owner, the reliable public money is in conservation and specialty, not commodities. Wheat pays through scale you don't want; lavender + conservation cover pays through programs sized for exactly your acreage.
Best of both

Farm a little, conserve the rest

Your plan already fits this: own 5–10 acres, farm only 2–3 in lavender. The other 2–7 acres are candidates for CRP/CREP rent, a pollinator easement, and current-use tax — earning money and tax relief while sitting quiet.

Bucket 1 · Buying the land

USDA beginning-farmer loans do the heavy lifting on the purchase

The Farm Service Agency (FSA) is the one lender built around people who've never owned a farm. "Beginning farmer" = farmed under 10 years and own no more than 30% of your county's average farm size — you'll qualify easily.

Flagship

Down Payment Loan

$300,150max FSA share · 1.75% fixed (Apr 2026)

You put 5% down, FSA finances up to 45% at a deeply subsidized rate, a bank covers the rest. Reserved for beginning, minority, and women farmers.

Larger buys

Direct Farm Ownership

~5.75%direct rate · larger loan ceiling

FSA lends directly for the whole purchase when a bank won't, into the hundreds of thousands. Pair with the Down Payment loan or use guarantees on a bank loan.

Start small

Microloans

$50K + $50Kownership + operating · light paperwork

Streamlined loans designed for specialty & direct-market operations — ideal for early plantings, a hoop house, or a wash-pack shed before the big purchase.

Why it matters: the 1.75% Down Payment loan is essentially free money against inflation — the single most valuable tool for a first-time buyer.

Bucket 2a · Paid to hold land in cover

The Conservation Reserve Program pays annual rent to keep sensitive acres green

CRP — the base program

A 10–15 year contract: you agree not to farm the enrolled acres and plant conservation cover instead. In return, an annual rental payment (set by county cropland rates) plus up to 50% cost-share to establish the cover.

PNW-tuned

CREP

Washington's salmon-habitat version along streams — higher incentive payments plus cost-share for riparian buffers.

Wildlife

SAFE

State Acres for Wildlife Enhancement — rent + establishment + maintenance for targeted habitat.

2026 sign-up windows
  • Continuous CRP — Feb 12 → Mar 20, 2026
  • General CRP — Mar 9 → Apr 17, 2026
  • Enroll through your local FSA office
The catch: CRP land generally must have a cropping history, and you commit to not farming it for a decade-plus. It fits the buffer acres of your parcel — not the ground you'll plant in lavender.

Bucket 2b · Selling development rights

A conservation easement turns "I'll never subdivide" into a cash payment

You keep owning and farming the land — you permanently give up the right to develop it, and get paid for that right. It also drops the land's market value, which lowers your purchase price and property tax.

Federal

ACEP–ALE

up to 50%of easement fair-market value, paid by NRCS

Agricultural Land Easements protect working farms. For easements ≤ $500K, paid as a lump sum or up to 10 annual installments.

Washington

Farm PAI + FPLA

State programs that pair a low-interest acquisition loan with easement funding to support buy-protect-sell deals — a route to access already-protected, more affordable farmland.

Local partners

Land trusts & districts

County conservation districts and land trusts (e.g. on the Peninsula) hold easements and often stack their own funds on top of the federal share.

Angle for you: buying a parcel that already carries an ag easement can be markedly cheaper — you're not paying for development potential you'd never use anyway.

Bucket 3 · The ongoing tax break

Washington's Open Space Taxation Act values farmland at farm value, not development value

Current-use assessment

Under Ch. 84.34 RCW, qualifying Farm & Agricultural land is assessed on what it earns as a farm — often a small fraction of its "highest and best use" (subdividable) value. That gap is your recurring tax saving, every year you stay enrolled.

In Clallam County
  • Generally 4+ acres in ag use (or a subdividable parcel)
  • An open-space category exists too, on a public-benefit rating system
  • Farm & Ag allows land to lie fallow toward future ag use, with a 7-year intent path
Why it's powerful

Unlike a one-time grant, this is a permanent annual discount on your biggest fixed cost. On a Dungeness-valley parcel where development value is high, the savings can be substantial and compound over decades.

Watch the exit: pull land out of the program and you owe back taxes (typically ~7 years) plus interest and a penalty. Enroll only land you intend to keep in ag — which is the whole point of your plan.

Bucket 4 · Grants for what you build & grow

Cost-share for infrastructure and pollinators; true grants for climate-smart work

USDA · NRCS

EQIP & CSP

Cost-share to install practices: efficient irrigation, hoop houses, pollinator & wildlife habitat, soil health. EQIP guarantees a $4,000 minimum payment; CSP layers ongoing stewardship payments up to ~$40K/yr. WA runs annual "batching" deadlines — get on the list early.

WA State Conservation Commission

Sustainable Farms & Fields

For climate-smart practices that store carbon or cut emissions: free on-farm consultation, a climate-smart farm plan, and financial assistance toward project costs. Application windows open in spring (FY27 ran Apr 15 → May 15, 2026).

WSDA

Specialty Crop Block Grant

$25K–$250K awards (up to 3 years) — and lavender qualifies as a horticultural / medicinal specialty crop. Catch: grants can't benefit a single farm's profit, so you access them through a nonprofit, co-op, or WSU project, not as a solo applicant.

Add-ons

Value-added & marketing

USDA Value-Added Producer Grants (turning lavender into oil, sachets, culinary products) and SARE research grants reward exactly the value-added, agritourism model you're building toward.

Takeaway: the winning move isn't one crop — it's a split: lavender on the workable acres (specialty & value-added grants) + conservation cover on the rest (rent + cost-share) + current-use tax over the whole parcel.

Putting it together

The recommended stack, in the order you'd actually use it

  • 1 · Buy with an FSA Down Payment loan — 5% down at 1.75%; consider a parcel that already carries an ag easement to cut the price.
  • 2 · Enroll the whole parcel in Current-Use taxation — lock in the permanent annual tax discount from year one.
  • 3 · Put buffer/streamside acres into CRP or CREP — annual rent + cost-share on ground you were never going to plant anyway.
  • 4 · Fund lavender infrastructure with EQIP — irrigation efficiency, pollinator habitat, high tunnels; guaranteed $4K floor.
  • 5 · Add Sustainable Farms & Fields — free farm plan + cost-share for the climate-smart practices you'd do regardless.
  • 6 · Layer Value-Added & Specialty Crop grants — via a co-op or WSU partner, once the value-added / agritourism side is live.
Sequencing note: loans and tax classification happen at purchase; conservation and practice grants roll in over the first 3–5 years. Nothing here requires choosing one program over another — they're designed to stack.

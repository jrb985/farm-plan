Growing the grower: Washington's small-farm support network

Money buys the land — but people teach you to farm it. This is the map of who in Washington will train you, walk your fields, diagnose your soil, and help decide what to plant and how to run it — most of it free or low-cost, and much of it aimed squarely at your Olympic Peninsula.

The front door

WSU is Washington's land-grant university — and Extension is its farm-help arm in every county

Land-grant universities were created to bring practical agricultural knowledge to ordinary farmers. That mandate runs through WSU Extension, which has an office in every Washington county. It's your single best starting point.

Built for you

Regional Small Farms Program

A WSU program dedicated to the Clallam–Jefferson–Kitsap tri-county — over 1,500 farms, roughly half of them new. Personalized help on production, insurance, and business questions.

Your exact region
Statewide reach

Agriculture & Food Systems

WSU's broader Extension network — specialists in crops, soils, pests, food safety, and marketing you can consult by phone, email, or field visit.

Your county office

WSU Clallam Extension

The local branch: workshops, Master Gardeners, and the connection point to every program in this deck.

Why this matters: nearly everything else — courses, advisors, labs, grants — is reachable through Extension. Get on their mailing list a decade before you buy, and you'll learn the landscape for free.

Learn before you leap

Cultivating Success™ — WSU's course ladder for turning a farm dream into a farm plan

A three-step sequence you can take online (with regional field trips), designed for exactly your situation: someone planning a small acreage they don't yet own. Scholarships cover the fee if it's a barrier.

Step 1

Explore Your Farm Dream

Reality-checks the idea: values, goals, quality of life, and whether the numbers can work — before you spend a dollar on land.

Step 2

Whole Farm Planning

Builds the actual plan: land, resources, enterprises, and a written whole-farm strategy tying it together.

Step 3

Agricultural Entrepreneurship

The business layer: marketing, budgets, and a real business plan for the value-added / agritourism side.

Also

Living on the Land

Shorter workshops for small-acreage stewardship — water, soil, pasture, and getting oriented to rural land.

Format

Online + October field trips

Virtual statewide classes with in-person regional networking each fall. Connects you to working farmers, not just slides.

Input on what & how you run it

People who will actually come to your land and advise on how it's run

This is the heart of your question. Several services provide one-on-one, on-the-ground advising — you keep control, they bring expertise on decisions like what to plant, how to price it, and how to set the place up.

Peninsula-local

North Olympic Peninsula RC&D — Agricultural Business Advising

Free, confidential one-on-one advising for local farms: business planning, financials, enterprise choices, and growth strategy tailored to Peninsula conditions.

Business side

WSU Small Business Development Center

A dedicated SBDC advisor for the North Olympic Peninsula helps with the business mechanics — cash flow, pricing, structure — at no charge.

Conservation side

Clallam Conservation District farm planner

Free farm-plan visits: they walk the property and advise on soil, water, drainage, and stewardship practices specific to your parcel.

Production side

WSU Small Farms specialists

Extension faculty who answer crop, pest, and production questions and can point you to trials relevant to what you want to grow.

The science bench behind you

Free and low-cost diagnostics, testing, and data — the university lab, on call

Free

Master Gardener plant clinics

WSU-trained volunteers answer plant, pest, and soil questions in person, by phone, or email — clinics in every county.

Lab

Plant Pest Diagnostic Clinic

WSU's official lab for disease diagnosis, pest ID, and management strategies when something's wrong you can't name.

Soil

Soil-test referrals

Extension points you to accredited labs and helps you read the results — foundational before you plant perennials.

Data · free

AgWeatherNet

WSU's 175+ station weather network: real-time temperature, soil, rain, and frost data plus irrigation and crop tools.

For a lavender/perennial farm: soil tests + AgWeatherNet frost data are exactly what de-risks site selection and planting timing on a specific Dungeness-valley parcel — and both are essentially free.

Test-drive farming first

Incubators & apprenticeships — learn the work on someone else's land before you buy yours

With a decade of runway, the highest-value move may be hands-on immersion: farm real ground, with training and infrastructure provided, before committing your own capital.

Viva Farms — Practicum in Sustainable Agriculture

A 9-month hands-on program (Apr–Nov) covering the essentials: training, land, infrastructure, and market access. Bilingual, in Western Washington.

Viva Farms — Business Incubator

Practicum graduates can lease land and infrastructure to launch a real farm business at low risk — a bridge between learning and ownership.

Mentorship

TOPP organic mentoring

Through Tilth Alliance and the Northwest Regional Center for Organic Transition, the USDA's Transition to Organic Partnership Program pairs you with an experienced mentor.

Fit for you: a season or two in a practicum during your runway years would teach the rhythm of a specialty operation without betting the farm — literally.

Where the university has input — on your terms

Western SARE puts a researcher on your team while you keep the reins

Farmer / Rancher Research & Education Grant

You (the producer) partner with a technical advisor to design a 1–3 year on-farm experiment — test a practice, measure it, and share the results. The grant funds the trial and the outreach.

Why it answers your question

This is the cleanest way to get university input on what and how you farm while staying in charge: the research happens on your land, on your question, with an expert alongside you.

WA coordinator

Chad Kruger, WSU

Washington's SARE state coordinator helps applicants plan projects and outreach. WSU also runs SARE professional-development programming statewide.

Example fit

A lavender variety trial, a pollinator-strip experiment, or a low-water irrigation test — each a legitimate SARE project that also improves your own operation.

Where Central Washington fits

CWU owns the business-of-food niche — a complement to WSU's field role

Central Washington University isn't a land-grant, so it doesn't run county Extension. Its contribution is on the business and innovation side — genuinely useful for the value-added, agritourism model you're pursuing.

CWU

Food & Agribusiness Innovation — minor / certificate

The only Washington institution offering certificates focused on the business side of food and ag: processing, packaging, distribution, sales, and marketing — with a sustainability and entrepreneurship lens.

CWU

Wildcat Neighborhood Farm

A production-focused teaching farm supplying campus dining — the applied, learn-by-doing side of the program.

Bottom line: lean on WSU Extension for what to grow and how to grow it on the Peninsula; consider CWU if you want structured coursework on turning lavender into a sellable product line and brand.

Selling it & belonging to it

Outreach that helps you sell the crop and join the community

Market channel

WSU Farm to Food Bank & local food distribution

WSU Extension buys Peninsula-grown produce, meat, and eggs for food banks and increasingly coordinates local supply to schools — a real early sales outlet.

Peer network

Tilth Producers of Washington

Statewide organic & sustainable growers' network — conferences, field days, and a community of farmers doing what you plan to.

Land & funds

Peninsula land trusts

North Olympic & Jefferson Land Trusts protect farmland and run the Olympic Peninsula Farmers Fund supporting local growers directly.

Agritourism note: the lavender-farm model lives or dies on foot traffic and brand — these networks, plus your regional farm-tour associations, are how a new Sequim farm gets on the map.
